NLPContributionGraph

Structuring Scholarly NLP Contributions in the Open Research Knowledge Graph

  • arXiv Paper

A Scholarly Contribution Graph

Since scientific literature is growing at a rapid rate and researchers today are faced with this publications deluge, it is increasingly tedious, if not practically impossible to keep up with the research progress even within one's own narrow discipline. The Open Research Knowledge Graph (ORKG) is posited as a solution to the problem of keeping track of research progress minus the cognitive overload that reading dozens of full papers impose. It aims to build a comprehensive knowledge graph that publishes the research contributions of scholarly publications per paper, where the contributions are interconnected via the graph even across papers. With the ORKG, papers are easier to find and compare. With the NLPContributionGraph Shared Task, we formalise the building of a scholarly contribution graph as an automated task for the mining of contributions from NLP scholarly literature.

Task Overview

In our task, the goal is to find a set of subject-predicate-object statements for the shallow semantic structuring of contributions in scholarly publications that are more or less generically applicable for Natural Language Processing research.

Increasingly, text mining initiatives are seeking out recipes or formulaic semantic patterns to automatically mine machine-actionable information from scholarly articles. We have created a surface semantically structured dataset of scholarly articles structured for their research contributions in the field of natural language processing focused on machine learning applications (NLP-ML) across various information extraction tasks to be integrable within the ORKG. To this end, we have (1) identified sentences in the full text of scholarly publications that reflect research contributions; (2) created structured (subject,predicate,object) triple annotations from these sentences by identifying mentions of the contribution candidate entities and their relations; and (3) grouped collections of such triples, that arise from either consecutive or non-consecutive sentences, under one of eight core information units, viz. Research Problem, Approach, Objective, Experimental Setup, Results, Tasks, Experiments, Ablation Analysis, and Baselines. These core information units are posited as thematic scripts that are triple statement collections.

For a detailed task background, more explanation on the dataset annotation methodology can be found in the paper.
